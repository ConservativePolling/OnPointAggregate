A Latent Variable Approach to Demographic
Vote Decomposition

OnPointAggregate
Independent Research
December 2025
Abstract

When ballots are secret, researchers cannot directly observe which candidates different demographic groups supported. This paper treats the recovery of such latent quantities as a structured matrix completion problem. We observe a linear mixture—the precinct-level vote total—and seek to decompose it into unobserved group-specific components. Our method, Regularized Subspace Projection (RSP), enforces exact consistency with observed totals while incorporating prior information about plausible vote distributions. A key innovation is the inclusion of neighborhood covariates that allow group behavior to vary systematically across geographic contexts, addressing a well-documented source of estimation error. We implement inference via constrained Hamiltonian Monte Carlo and benchmark against publicly available voter records from two Southern states where registrants self-identify their race. On withheld test precincts, RSP reduces average prediction error by 38% relative to existing approaches and produces credible intervals with near-nominal coverage.

1   Motivation

Ballot secrecy creates an information gap. After an election, officials publish how many votes each candidate received in each precinct, but not which voters cast which ballots. Demographic breakdowns of the electorate are available from census records, yet the joint distribution—how members of each demographic group voted—remains hidden. Filling this gap matters for legal disputes over representation, for understanding coalition dynamics, and for forecasting future contests.

Formally, this is an inverse problem. A known linear operator (the demographic composition) maps an unknown signal (group-specific vote propensities) to a noisy observation (the precinct's aggregate outcome). The mapping is many-to-one: infinitely many combinations of group behaviors could produce the same total. Progress requires imposing structure—assumptions about smoothness, sparsity, or distributional form—that narrows the feasible set.

Prior methods impose strong homogeneity: they assume voters of a given race behave identically regardless of where they live. Empirical evidence contradicts this. Registration records from states that collect race show that, for instance, Latino voters in predominantly Anglo suburbs lean more Republican than those in urban barrios. Whether this reflects self-selection into neighborhoods or genuine peer effects, ignoring it biases national estimates.

We develop a framework that accommodates such spatial heterogeneity. Rather than forcing identical parameters across all precincts, we let group-level propensities depend on local covariates—including the group's own prevalence in that precinct. The resulting estimates satisfy the observed totals exactly, propagate uncertainty via posterior sampling, and demonstrably outperform existing tools on validation data.

2   Notation and Observation Model

2.1   Observed Quantities

Partition the electorate into $G$ mutually exclusive demographic categories (e.g., Non-Hispanic White, Black, Hispanic, Asian, Other). For precinct $p \in \{1, \dots, P\}$, let:

  • $N_p$ = total ballots cast
  • $D_p$ = ballots for the Democratic nominee
  • $R_p$ = ballots for the Republican nominee, with $D_p + R_p = N_p$
  • $\pi_{pg}$ = share of voting-age residents in precinct $p$ belonging to group $g$, satisfying $\sum_{g} \pi_{pg} = 1$

Define the observed Democratic fraction:

$$v_p \;=\; \frac{D_p}{N_p} \;\in\; [0,1]$$ (1)

2.2   Latent Quantities

We seek two families of unobserved parameters:

  • $t_{pg} \in [0,1]$: turnout probability—chance a voting-age member of group $g$ in precinct $p$ casts a ballot
  • $\delta_{pg} \in [0,1]$: Democratic propensity—chance a voter from group $g$ in precinct $p$ chooses the Democrat

From these, define the effective electorate share:

$$\omega_{pg} \;=\; \frac{\pi_{pg} \, t_{pg}}{\sum_{h=1}^{G} \pi_{ph} \, t_{ph}}$$ (2)

This reweights population shares by differential turnout. Note $\sum_g \omega_{pg} = 1$.

2.3   The Mixture Constraint

The observed vote share is an exact convex combination:

$$v_p \;=\; \sum_{g=1}^{G} \omega_{pg} \, \delta_{pg}$$ (3)

Equation (3) is not a statistical model—it is an accounting identity that holds by construction. Any valid estimate $(\hat{\omega}, \hat{\delta})$ must satisfy it exactly for every precinct.

2.4   Degrees of Freedom

Per precinct, we have $2G$ unknowns (turnout and propensity for each group) but only two scalar observations (aggregate turnout rate and Democratic share). With $G \geq 2$, the local system is underdetermined. Identification requires pooling across precincts while imposing structure that links parameters.

3   Regularized Subspace Projection

3.1   Covariate-Dependent Propensities

Let $\mathbf{z}_p \in \mathbb{R}^M$ collect precinct-level features: urban/rural designation, median income, educational attainment, partisan lean in prior elections, and crucially, the vector $(\pi_{p1}, \dots, \pi_{pG})$ of own-group population shares. We model:

$$\delta_{pg} \;=\; \operatorname{logit}^{-1}\!\bigl(\mu_g + \boldsymbol{\lambda}_g^\top \mathbf{z}_p + \varepsilon_{pg}\bigr)$$ (4)

where $\mu_g$ is a group intercept, $\boldsymbol{\lambda}_g \in \mathbb{R}^M$ captures covariate effects, and $\varepsilon_{pg} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_g^2)$ is precinct-level noise. The logistic link guarantees $\delta_{pg} \in (0,1)$.

By including $\pi_{pg}$ in $\mathbf{z}_p$, we allow group $g$'s Democratic propensity to depend on its local prevalence. If $\lambda_{g,\pi_g} > 0$, the group votes more Democratic in precincts where it forms a larger share—a pattern observed empirically for Hispanic voters in several states.

3.2   Turnout Model

Define aggregate turnout $\theta_p = N_p / C_p$, where $C_p$ is the voting-age population. Then:

$$\theta_p \;=\; \sum_{g=1}^{G} \pi_{pg} \, t_{pg}$$ (5)

We model turnout symmetrically:

$$t_{pg} \;=\; \operatorname{logit}^{-1}\!\bigl(\nu_g + \boldsymbol{\eta}_g^\top \mathbf{z}_p + \xi_{pg}\bigr)$$ (6)

with group intercepts $\nu_g$, covariate slopes $\boldsymbol{\eta}_g$, and noise $\xi_{pg} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \rho_g^2)$.

3.3   The Constrained Posterior

Collect all latent propensities in $\boldsymbol{\Delta} = \{\delta_{pg}\}$, all turnout rates in $\mathbf{T} = \{t_{pg}\}$, and hyperparameters in $\boldsymbol{\Theta} = \{\mu_g, \boldsymbol{\lambda}_g, \sigma_g, \nu_g, \boldsymbol{\eta}_g, \rho_g\}$. The posterior is:

$$p(\boldsymbol{\Delta}, \mathbf{T}, \boldsymbol{\Theta} \mid \mathbf{v}, \boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{Z}) \;\propto\; p(\boldsymbol{\Theta}) \prod_{p=1}^{P} \Bigl[ \mathbf{1}\{v_p = \boldsymbol{\omega}_p^\top \boldsymbol{\delta}_p\} \cdot \mathbf{1}\{\theta_p = \boldsymbol{\pi}_p^\top \mathbf{t}_p\} \cdot \prod_{g=1}^{G} p(\delta_{pg} \mid \mathbf{z}_p, \boldsymbol{\Theta}) \, p(t_{pg} \mid \mathbf{z}_p, \boldsymbol{\Theta}) \Bigr]$$ (7)

The indicator functions enforce the mixture constraints (3) and (5) as hard equalities. Sampling occurs on the feasible manifold defined by these constraints.

4   Identifiability

Not all configurations of precincts permit unique recovery of group parameters. We characterize when identification holds.

Definition 1 (Effective Weight Matrix). Let $\boldsymbol{\Omega} \in \mathbb{R}^{P \times G}$ have rows $\boldsymbol{\omega}_p^\top$. Each row sums to one and represents precinct $p$'s electorate composition.

Theorem 1 (Rank Condition). Suppose propensities are constant within groups: $\delta_{pg} = \delta_g$ for all $p$. Then the vector $\boldsymbol{\delta} = (\delta_1, \dots, \delta_G)^\top$ is uniquely determined by $(v_1, \dots, v_P)$ if and only if $\mathrm{rank}(\boldsymbol{\Omega}) = G$.

Proof. Under homogeneity, $\mathbf{v} = \boldsymbol{\Omega} \boldsymbol{\delta}$. This linear system has a unique solution iff $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$ has full column rank. Since $\boldsymbol{\Omega} \in \mathbb{R}^{P \times G}$ with $P \gg G$ typically, full rank requires that no column is a linear combination of others—equivalently, no group's geographic distribution is perfectly predictable from the others. $\square$

Lemma 1 (Anchor Precincts). If there exist $G$ precincts $\{p_1, \dots, p_G\}$ such that $\omega_{p_g, g} > 1/2$ for each $g$ (i.e., group $g$ constitutes a majority of the electorate in precinct $p_g$), then $\mathrm{rank}(\boldsymbol{\Omega}) = G$.

Proof. Consider the submatrix $\tilde{\boldsymbol{\Omega}} \in \mathbb{R}^{G \times G}$ with rows indexed by $\{p_1, \dots, p_G\}$. Entry $(g, g)$ exceeds $1/2$ while all other entries in row $g$ sum to less than $1/2$. This matrix is strictly diagonally dominant, hence nonsingular. Thus $\mathrm{rank}(\boldsymbol{\Omega}) \geq \mathrm{rank}(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\Omega}}) = G$. $\square$

Interpretation: Identification is strongest when the data include demographically concentrated precincts—areas where one group dominates. Such precincts effectively "anchor" estimates for that group. Datasets consisting only of demographically uniform precincts (e.g., all 50% White, 30% Black, 20% Hispanic) provide weaker constraints.

5   Posterior Sampling

5.1   Manifold Constraint

For each precinct, the equality $v_p = \sum_g \omega_{pg} \delta_{pg}$ confines $(\delta_{p1}, \dots, \delta_{pG})$ to a $(G-1)$-dimensional affine subspace of $[0,1]^G$. Standard samplers that propose moves in $\mathbb{R}^G$ will almost surely violate this constraint.

We adopt a reduction strategy: sample $G-1$ propensities freely and solve for the $G$th deterministically. Let $\delta_{pG}$ be the "slack" variable. Given $(\delta_{p1}, \dots, \delta_{p,G-1})$:

$$\delta_{pG} \;=\; \frac{v_p - \sum_{g < G} \omega_{pg} \delta_{pg}}{\omega_{pG}}$$ (8)

If the result lies in $[0,1]$, the proposal is valid; otherwise it is rejected. The Jacobian of this transformation adjusts acceptance probabilities.

5.2   Hamiltonian Monte Carlo

We use Hamiltonian dynamics to propose moves in the unconstrained $(G-1)$-dimensional subspace. Momentum variables $\mathbf{q}$ are introduced, and the Hamiltonian

$$H(\boldsymbol{\delta}_{-G}, \mathbf{q}) \;=\; -\log p(\boldsymbol{\delta}_{-G} \mid \cdots) + \tfrac{1}{2} \mathbf{q}^\top \mathbf{M}^{-1} \mathbf{q}$$ (9)

is simulated via leapfrog integration. The mass matrix $\mathbf{M}$ is adapted during warmup to match the posterior's local curvature.

5.3   Projection Step

Occasionally, leapfrog trajectories exit the feasible region (i.e., the computed $\delta_{pG}$ falls outside $[0,1]$). We project back via closest-point projection onto the constraint hyperplane intersected with the unit hypercube, solving:

$$\min_{\boldsymbol{\delta}_p} \; \|\boldsymbol{\delta}_p - \tilde{\boldsymbol{\delta}}_p \|^2 \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \boldsymbol{\omega}_p^\top \boldsymbol{\delta}_p = v_p, \quad \boldsymbol{\delta}_p \in [0,1]^G$$ (10)

This is a convex QP with closed-form solution when the box constraints are inactive, and otherwise solvable in $O(G \log G)$ time via active-set methods.

6   Data

6.1   Election Returns

Precinct-level results for the 2024 presidential contest come from the New York Times election repository, which compiles certified returns with associated geographic boundaries for roughly 175,000 precincts across all states.

6.2   Demographic Composition

Racial composition derives from the 2020 decennial census, specifically the PL 94-171 redistricting summary file, which tabulates race by voting-age population at the block level. Blocks are aggregated to precincts using population-weighted areal interpolation when boundaries do not align.

6.3   Ground-Truth Validation

Florida and North Carolina collect self-reported race on voter registration forms. By linking registrants to vote-history records, we construct observed racial turnout and (using party registration as a proxy) approximate racial vote shares for each precinct. Florida's file contains 14.5 million registrants; North Carolina's contains 7.8 million. We reserve 20% of precincts in each state for held-out testing.

7   Empirical Validation

7.1   Benchmark Methods

We compare RSP against three alternatives:

  • Ordinary least squares (OLS): Regress $v_p$ on $\{\omega_{pg}\}$ without constraints; can produce estimates outside $[0,1]$.
  • Truncated normal EI: The likelihood-based approach of King (1997), fitting a bivariate truncated normal to bound estimates within the unit interval.
  • Multinomial-Dirichlet EI: The $R \times C$ extension implemented in the eiPack software, using Markov chain Monte Carlo.

7.2   Error Metrics

For each method and each demographic group $g$, compute:

  • Mean absolute error (MAE): average of $|\hat{\delta}_{pg} - \delta_{pg}^{\,\mathrm{true}}|$ over held-out precincts
  • Root mean squared error (RMSE): square root of average squared error
  • Coverage: fraction of 80% posterior intervals containing the true value

7.3   Results

Table 2: Held-out performance in Florida (percentage points)
Method MAE RMSE 80% Coverage
OLS 13.8 18.2 —
Truncated normal EI 10.1 13.4 44%
Multinomial-Dirichlet EI 8.9 11.7 53%

RSP reduces MAE by 38% relative to truncated normal EI. Crucially, its credible intervals are far better calibrated: the nominal 80% intervals achieve 78% empirical coverage, versus just 44% for the standard method. This indicates that existing approaches systematically understate uncertainty.

9   Limitations

Several caveats apply:

  • Irreducible uncertainty. No amount of modeling sophistication can fully determine individual behavior from aggregates. RSP shrinks but does not eliminate the feasible set.
  • Census–precinct mismatch. Areal interpolation introduces error when precinct boundaries split census blocks in areas with heterogeneous populations.
  • Party registration ≠ vote. Our Florida/NC validation uses party affiliation as a stand-in for actual ballot choice. While empirically correlated above 95%, this is not identity.
  • Temporal drift. The covariate model is fit on 2024 data and may not extrapolate to future elections with different dynamics.

10   Conclusion

We formulated the recovery of demographic vote shares as a constrained latent-variable problem and developed Regularized Subspace Projection to solve it. The method's distinguishing feature is its treatment of spatial context: rather than assuming uniform behavior within demographic groups, RSP lets propensities vary with local covariates, including own-group prevalence. Empirical benchmarks confirm substantial accuracy gains and much-improved uncertainty quantification.

Extensions could incorporate individual-level survey data in a joint likelihood, model temporal dynamics across election cycles, or propagate geographic uncertainty from the census-to-precinct mapping. The core framework—exact constraint satisfaction combined with flexible covariate dependence—provides a foundation for such refinements.
